Challenges - commodity calculator
  1. a) How much copper would be needed to provide every person between the ages of 10 and 39, in Australia in 2010, with a mobile phone?

    b) What is the main mineral from which copper is extracted in Australia?

    c) Where are the major copper mines located in Australia?

  2. a) How much copper would be needed to provide every person between the ages of 10 and 39, in Asia in 2010 with a mobile phone?

    b) Where in the world are the major copper mines located?

  3. What mass of titanium would be needed to provide every student in your school with a bicycle, like the one shown in the commodity calculator?

  4. Would more aluminium be needed to supply every person between the ages of 20 and 39 in America in 2010 with a refrigerator or to supply every person in Asia between the ages of 10 - 19 with a scooter?

  5. The price of iron could increase significantly. This would have a direct impact on the cost of some consumer products.

    a) Which consumer products listed in the calculator would be expected to increase in price as a consequence of
    an increased cost for the iron?

    b) For which product would the price increase be most significant and why would this be so?

  6. Iron ore mined in the Pilbara varies in composition from 50 to 69% of iron by mass. How much high grade ore (69% iron) would need to be mined to enable every Australian aged between 30 - 39 in 2005, to own a television?

  7. Aluminium is used in the manufacture of the car, mobile phone, bike and the TV. Bauxite is the ore that is mined to supply the aluminium. The Bauxite refined to produce alumina which is then smelted to make aluminium. This process requires large amounts of energy. Approximately 25% of bauxite is aluminium.

    a) How much aluminium would be needed to enable every person aged 20 - 29 in the world, in 2005, to have their own mobile phone, bicycle and television?

    b) How much bauxite would need to be mined to produce this much aluminium?

    c) How much energy would be needed to extract this aluminium from the bauxite if the energy demand for this task is 10.000 kWh/tonne?

  8. With advancing technology computers become obsolete faster, their parts have less value and the need for recycling grows.

    a) Identify the main metals which are used in the manufacture of computers.

    b) Make a list of other household objects which could be manufactured from the metals extracted and recycled from obsolete computers.

    c) Conduct research to identify which countries have an effective program for the recycling of metals from computers.

  9. The commodity calculator can be used to identify the demand for different metals. Look at the metal analysis for a mobile phone.

    a) Conduct research to find out how much of each component metal is produced in Australia, per year.

    b) Using 2004 production figures, did Australia produce enough of each of the component metals, to provide every Australian aged between 20 and 29 in 2005, with a mobile phone?

  10. Aluminium can also be obtained by the recycling of aluminium cans, foil, dishes, saucepans and garden furniture. Identify the advantages in creating car parts from recycled aluminium rather than extracting the aluminium from bauxite.

  11. The chemical composition of the objects listed in the commodity calculator is an approximation only. The totals do not add to 100 per cent as the non-metals present in the object are not identified. Which parts of the modern car are likely to be made from non-metals?

  12. Magnesium is being used in the manufacture of modern cars to make the structure lighter and more fuel efficient. Calculate how much lighter the car would be if all of the aluminium was replaced by magnesium.

  13. Copper is a necessary part of so many modern consumer products that the amount of copper consumed is a good indicator of a country's economic health. As the economy grows, copper consumption grows along with it.

    a) Which of the products listed in the commodity calculator contain copper?

    b) Which of the consumer products found in the home contain copper?

    c) In which parts of the world is the demand for these consumer products likely to increase by 2010?

    d) What properties of copper make it a metal which is in such high demand?

    e) Each year in the USA almost as much copper is mined as is obtained by recycling. Why is copper so easy to recycle?
