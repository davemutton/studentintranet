Australia's Minerals

Ever since humans first appeared on Earth they have used the earth’s natural materials or resources, to meet their daily needs. Water, plants, animals, timber, minerals and metals are examples of these natural materials. These provide us with food, clothing, shelter and a means of transportation.

As technologies have developed, so too has our use of these natural resources increased. Many of the materials we use every day come from the Earth’s crust.

Today, mineral resources are used for a variety of agricultural, industrial and other purposes.

Mineral resources such as quartz and feldspar (sandstone), calcite (limestone) and gypsum are used for building materials. Some mineral resources, such as gold and diamonds, are found in their pure form. However, most of the metals we use today come from ore minerals from which we have to extract the metals by the processes of refining and smelting.


Australia – Rich in Minerals and Energy Sources

Minerals are mined in every state of Australia. Metals such as aluminium, copper, gold, iron, lead, manganese, nickel, silver, tin, titanium and zinc are all formed from minerals.

Coal is mined extensively in eastern Australia and is exported to about 40 different countries around the world.

Many products are made from Australian petroleum and they include oil, gas, petrol and diesel. Some of Australian petroleum fields are found on land while others are located offshore.

Uranium is an energy source also found and mined in Australia. The uranium mined in Australia is not used locally, rather is sold overseas where it is used to fuel nuclear power stations. Most of Australia’s electricity is generated in power stations fuelled by coal or gas. Currently, about 2% of Australia’s energy requirements are produced from renewable sources of fuel such as hydro, solar and wind energy.

Australia produces most of the world’s precious opals and is a major supplier of diamonds and sapphires. Australia also supplies building and industrial materials that are used for a wide variety of purposes. These include: basalt, clay, corundum, diamonds, diatomite, garnet, granite, gravel, gypsum, limestone, magnetite, marble, mica, salt, sand, sandstone, scoria, slate, talc and many more.


Australian Minerals Industry

The task of searching for, mining and processing Australia’s mineral and energy sources is undertaken by the Australian minerals industry.

In Australia, this industry is one of considerable size and economic and social significance. It benefits all Australians both directly and indirectly. The Australian minerals industry:

  • accounts directly for around 8.5 per cent of Gross Domestic Product (GDP);

  • is the world’s third largest minerals sector by value of production (after the US and South Africa);

  • is the world’s largest exporter of gold, iron ore and black coal;

  • is the world’s leading producer of bauxite and alumina, diamonds (by volume), ilmenite, rutile and zircon and tantalum.

  • is the second largest producer of zinc ore.

  • is the third largest producer of aluminium, iron ore, nickel and gold, and the fifth largest coal producer.

  • has contributed, through exports, some $500 billion directly to Australia’s wealth over the past 20 years;

  • in 2002-03 generated exports of around $42 billion, representing approximately 35 per cent of Australia’s total merchandise exports and 28 per cent of total exports of goods and services;

  • is responsible for producing and exporting 60% of the mining software used in operations around the word ( worth approximately $2 billion pa);

  • directly and indirectly employs some 321,000 Australians with much of this in sparsely populated, remote and regional Australia.
